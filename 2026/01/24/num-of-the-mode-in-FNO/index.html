The Cause of Aliasing

Author: zhongyan ouyang Date: January 24, 2026  15:19:15 Category: machine learning

Aliasing is not a numerical error or an engineering approximation problem, but a structural consequence of the mathematical non-invertibility of the uniform sampling linear operator. The fundamental reason is: once a continuous function is discretely sampled, the frequency space loses its original discriminative capacity and is compressed into a quotient space, rendering different continuous frequencies completely indistinguishable under discrete observation.

Let $f(x)$ be a periodic function defined on the interval $[0,1]$, sampled at $N$ equally spaced points:

$$
x_j = \frac{j}{N}, \quad j = 0, \dots, N-1.
$$

This defines the linear sampling operator parameterized by $\mathbb{N}$:

$$
\mathcal S_N: f \longmapsto \big(f(x_0),\dots,f(x_{N-1})\big)\in\mathbb C^N.
$$

Since the target space $\mathbb C^N$ is finite-dimensional while the space of continuous functions (or its Fourier expansion space) is infinite-dimensional, the operator $\mathcal S_N$ cannot be injective (单射) (Why?). Therefore, its kernel must be non-trivial.

Definition: The kernel of an operator is a set, which includes all the elements that can be mapped to zero by this operator. A non-trivial kernel means there exist nonzero elements that can be mapped to zero. A trivial kernel is a set only includes the zero element.

Aliasing originates precisely from this non-trivial kernel, not from empirical reasons like insufficient sampling precision.

To clarify the structure of the kernel, consider the continuous Fourier modes:

$$
\phi_k(x)=e^{2\pi i k x},\quad k\in\mathbb Z.
$$

At the sampling points, their values are:

$$
\phi_k(x_j)=e^{2\pi i k j/N}.
$$

Here, a decisive identity emerges:

$$
e^{2\pi i (k+mN) j/N}=e^{2\pi i k j/N},\quad \forall j,\ \forall m\in\mathbb Z.
$$

This identity directly implies:

$$
\mathcal S_N(\phi_{k+mN})=\mathcal S_N(\phi_k),
$$

Remark: Here briefly explain these equations. The function $\phi$ can be regarded as a feature extraction, or a mapping that the input is belongs to time domain, and the output is belongs to the frequency domain. These function is parameterised by k. The relation involving the operator $\mathcal S_N$ reveals a fundamental insight: Two distinct continuous functions can produce identical discrete samples after uniform sampling.

or equivalently:

$$
\phi_{k+mN}-\phi_k \in \ker(\mathcal S_N).
$$

Remark This relationship follows from the linearity of the sampling operator.

Therefore, all frequencies that differ by integer multiples of $N$ are strictly indistinguishable under discrete sampling. This conclusion is not an approximation but is exactly guaranteed by the periodicity of the exponential function.

From this, the set of integer frequencies $\mathbb Z$ (which contains the parameter $k$ of feature mapping function $\phi$) is partitioned into equivalence classes under the sampling operation:

$$
[k]={k+mN\mid m\in\mathbb Z}.
$$

After sampling, each equivalence class can only correspond to one discrete degree of freedom. From an algebraic perspective, the frequency space is compressed into the quotient space:

$$
\mathbb Z / N\mathbb Z.
$$

Definition: Given vector space $V$ and subspace $W \subset V$, the quotient $V/W$ is defined as:$$
V/W=\lbrace[v] \mid v \in V \rbrace

$$
where the equivalence classes is $[v]=v+W={ v+w \mid w \in W }$. The quotient space constructs new space by identifying equivalent elements.

To avoid the same discrete degree of freedom being represented multiple times, one must choose a canonical (最简洁的) representative set in this quotient space. When $N$ is even, the most natural choice is the so-called Nyquist interval:
$$

\left\lbrace-\frac{N}{2}+1,\dots,\frac{N}{2}\right\rbrace.

$$
This interval is not an empirical “frequency cutoff” but rather a fundamental domain of the frequency quotient space.

The “folding formula” commonly seen in engineering and numerical analysis can also be derived naturally from this. Given any frequency $k\in\mathbb Z$, one can always find some integer $m$ such that:
$$

k_{\text{alias}} = k - mN

$$
satisfies:
$$

|k_{\text{alias}}|\le \frac{N}{2}.

$$
The so-called “high frequency folding into low frequency” is not a physical process, but simply choosing a representative element within an equivalence class.

From a linear algebra perspective, this structure is equally inevitable. The space spanned by continuous Fourier modes is infinite-dimensional, while the sampled discrete signal space $\mathbb C^N$ has only $N$ degrees of freedom. Therefore, any discrete representation can carry at most $N$ independent pieces of information. Modes beyond the Nyquist frequency cannot introduce new identifiable degrees of freedom; they can only reparameterize existing discrete information. In statistics and learning theory, this corresponds to non-identifiable parameters.

In summary, the precise mathematical cause of aliasing can be stated as: The uniform sampling operator induces a quotient space structure on the frequency space, making Fourier modes that differ by integer multiples of the sampling rate completely equivalent under discrete observation. The Nyquist frequency is not an empirical rule, but the maximal canonical representative interval available in this quotient space. Any frequency exceeding the Nyquist limit does not correspond to a new physical or mathematical degree of freedom, but merely represents an alternative parameterization of existing low-frequency information. This fact forms the rigorous mathematical foundation for frequency truncation principles in spectral methods and Fourier Neural Operators.
